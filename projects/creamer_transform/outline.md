# Creamer Transform Visualization Outline

## Project Goal

Build a Manim project that explains the Creamer transform with the right emphasis:

- the main story is the 1989 deep-water construction
- the transform is a better canonical coordinate map, not a new wave equation
- the physical nonlinearity is shifted from the truncated dynamics into the reconstruction map
- finite depth should be introduced as a genuine change of regime, not as a cosmetic extension

The presentation should help the viewer answer three questions:

1. What problem was Creamer trying to solve?
2. Why does deep water admit such a clean picture?
3. What changes when we try to do the same thing at finite depth?

---

## Core Interpretation To Preserve

The animation should keep returning to the same conceptual spine:

- start from Zakharov variables `(\eta, \phi_s)`
- expand the Hamiltonian as `H = H_2 + H_3 + H_4 + ...`
- in deep water, three-wave resonances are absent
- therefore the cubic Hamiltonian `H_3` is non-resonant and can be removed by a near-identity canonical transform
- after that transform, the retained evolution can be treated as linear to the target order
- but the physical surface is still nonlinear because reconstructing the Eulerian surface from the transformed variables is itself nonlinear

This is the key intellectual point:

`Creamer does not erase bound harmonics; it relocates their origin.`

They no longer appear as leading nonlinear dynamics in the reduced Hamiltonian.
They reappear as bound structure generated by the nonlinear map back to the physical surface.

## Narrative Structure

## Scene 0: The Question

Goal: establish what the method is trying to improve.

- Start with the usual free-surface variables `(\eta, \phi_s)`.
- Show the Hamiltonian split `H_2 + H_3 + H_4 + ...`.
- Mark `H_3` as the first nonlinear obstruction in the retained dynamics.
- State the target clearly:
  `Can we keep the same water-wave physics, but move to better variables?`
- Contrast:
  - Stokes-style expansion around the flat `z=0`
  - shape-function / DNO expansion that can follow a long-wave reference shape
- End with a bridge card, not a full answer:
  - in deep water, Creamer first chooses to absorb only `H_3`
  - next question: why is `H_3` the first target, and why does deep water allow that?

Takeaway:

- Creamer is a coordinate-choice story inside Hamiltonian water-wave theory, and the deep-water story begins by singling out `H_3`.

## Scene 1: Why `H_3` Is the First Target

Goal: explain why `H_3` is the first target and why deep water makes that target removable, without yet diving fully into the generator `W`.

Recommended internal structure:

- Beat 1: why start with `H_3`?
  - `H_2` is the quadratic / linear propagation part
  - `H_3` is the first genuinely nonlinear correction
  - if I want a "better representation" at the lowest nonlinear order, `H_3` is the first thing to inspect

- Beat 2: triad coupling is not the same thing as triad resonance
  - show `k_1 + k_2 = k_3` as geometric closure
  - separately ask whether `\omega_1 + \omega_2 = \omega_3`
  - make clear that closure alone does not force dynamical importance

- Beat 3: deep water is special
  - emphasize `\omega^2 = g|k|`
  - visually test the three-wave condition and show that exact deep-water triad resonance fails
  - therefore the cubic interaction is present, but non-resonant

- Beat 4: consequence
  - because `H_3` is non-resonant, it is removable rather than structurally forced to stay
  - end with a teaser:
    `if {H_2, W} = H_3, then the transformed Hamiltonian starts as K = H_2 + O(4)`
  - but keep the actual construction of `W` for Scene 2
  - do not start from the full generating functional here

Takeaway:

- Deep water is special because the first nonlinear Hamiltonian is present but non-resonant, so the deep-water story naturally starts by trying to absorb `H_3`.

## Scene 2: The Canonical Transform Idea

Goal: explain what Creamer actually constructs, but in the lightest possible way.

- Start from the question:
  `what change of variables could remove a non-resonant cubic term?`
- Introduce the near-identity canonical transform.
- Return to the teaser from Scene 1 and now show the symbolic cancellation:
  `{H_2, W} = H_3`.
- Make the transformed Hamiltonian collapse to `K = H_2 + O(4)`.
- Stress that the transformed variables are still canonical.
- Emphasize that this is why Scene 1 only needed to establish non-resonance first:
  removability is a dynamical statement that motivates the transform.
- Treat the global generating functional as background context only:
  - mention that the paper presents it first
  - but do not make it the pedagogical center of the animation
- Use the Lie-transform viewpoint only as much as needed to explain the bookkeeping:
  - `\partial_\lambda A = \{A, W\}`
  - `\lambda` is not physical time
  - it is just the cleanest way to express the canonical deformation

Takeaway:

- The method simplifies the Hamiltonian, not the physical ocean itself.

## Scene 3: Deep-Water 1D Miracle

Goal: show why the 1D deep-water form is especially intuitive.

- Narrow from the general canonical transform to the 1D deep-water specialization.
- Introduce the Hilbert transform and the simplified characteristic picture.
- Present the coordinate remapping idea:
  a point labeled by parameter `\chi` is shifted horizontally before being read in Eulerian `x`.
- Show the formula schematically as
  `x = \chi + displacement(\chi)`.

Takeaway:

- In 1D deep water, the abstract canonical transform becomes geometrically readable as a horizontal remapping of surface points.

## Scene 4: Reconstruction Creates Bound Structure

Goal: make the physical payoff visible.

- Start from a simple parent wave in parameter space.
- Keep the transformed evolution simple.
- Then remap points horizontally and read the profile back in Eulerian space.
- Animate crest sharpening, trough flattening, and the appearance of higher harmonics.
- Make it explicit that these extra harmonics are not newly propagating free modes.

Takeaway:

- The nonlinear surface shape is generated during reconstruction.
- Bound harmonics are geometric output of the map, not separate free-wave dynamics.

## Scene 5: Why This Matters Physically

Goal: connect the transform to Creamer's physical usefulness.

- Show short waves riding on long waves in the transformed picture.
- Interpret packet shift, straining, and amplitude modulation as consequences of remapping.
- Emphasize why this is attractive:
  a simpler evolution law plus a physically rich reconstruction.

Takeaway:

- The transform is powerful because it captures important nonlinear kinematics while keeping the retained dynamics unusually simple.

## Scene 6: What Finite Depth Changes

Goal: pivot carefully to the finite-depth question without overselling continuity.

- Begin with:
  `Finite depth is not just deep water with tanh(kh) inserted everywhere.`
- Show the deep-water logic on the left and finite-depth concerns on the right.
- List the regime changes:
  - the clean deep-water geometric picture weakens
  - the transform still exists, but its form is less transparent
  - near-resonant or resonant three-wave structure becomes more relevant as depth decreases
- Distinguish:
  - removable non-resonant cubic structure
  - near-resonant small-denominator structure
  - genuinely resonant structure that should stay in the dynamics

Takeaway:

- Finite depth is a different design problem: not only "what transform exists," but "what should be absorbed into the mapping and what must remain dynamical?"

## Scene 7: A Practical Finite-Depth Research Agenda

Goal: end with a forward-looking outline for your project.

- Pose the finite-depth question in practical rather than purely formal terms.
- Candidate targets:
  - best crest-trough reconstruction
  - best short-on-long modulation
  - best reduced-error initialization for simulations
  - best separation between bound and free content
- Emphasize that the right finite-depth object may be:
  - a canonical normal-form transform
  - a depth-dependent reconstruction map
  - or a hybrid of both

Takeaway:

- The deep-water Creamer story is a solved conceptual template.
- Finite depth is where the research question becomes open and interesting.

---

## Recommended Emphasis

If runtime is limited, spend most of the screen time here:

1. Why `H_3` is removable in deep water
2. Why the 1D deep-water case becomes a geometric remapping
3. Why bound harmonics survive as reconstruction output

Keep finite depth shorter and more strategic:

- explain why it is not trivial
- explain what breaks
- explain what the next research question should be

---

## Visual Priorities

- Use one consistent color for physical variables `(\eta, \phi_s)`.
- Use another consistent color for transformed/linear variables.
- Treat the map from parameter space to Eulerian space as the central visual motif.
- Prefer showing point redistribution, not only equations.
- When discussing bound harmonics, always pair shape change with a spectral cue.
- Avoid presenting the transform as "magic cancellation"; always tie it to non-resonance.
